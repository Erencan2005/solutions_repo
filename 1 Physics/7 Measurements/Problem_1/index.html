Problem 1: Measuring Earth's Gravitational Acceleration with a Pendulum

🔬 Motivation

The acceleration due to gravity, \(g\), is a fundamental constant that influences a wide range of physical phenomena. Measuring \(g\) is crucial for understanding gravitational interactions, designing structures, and conducting experiments in various fields. A simple pendulum provides a classical method for determining \(g\), as its period depends on the local gravitational field.

🎯 Task

Measure the acceleration due to gravity (\(g\)) using a pendulum and analyze the uncertainties in the measurement.

🧪 Procedure

Materials

  • A string (~1 to 1.5 meters long)
  • A small weight (e.g., a metal bob, coin, bag of sugar, key chain)
  • Stopwatch (or smartphone timer)
  • Ruler or measuring tape

Steps

  1. Attach the weight to the string and fix the other end to a sturdy support.
  2. Measure the length of the pendulum, \(L\), from the suspension point to the center of the weight using a ruler or measuring tape.
  3. Record the resolution of the measuring tool.
  4. Calculate the uncertainty in length:
    $$ \Delta L = \frac{\text{Ruler Resolution}}{2} $$
  5. Displace the pendulum slightly (~15°) and release it.
  6. Measure the time for 10 full oscillations (\(T_{10}\)). Repeat this 10 times and record all values.
  7. Calculate the mean time for 10 oscillations, \(\overline{T_{10}}\), and the standard deviation, \(\sigma_{T_{10}}\).
  8. Determine the uncertainty in the mean time: $$ \Delta \overline{T_{10}} = \frac{\sigma_{T_{10}}}{\sqrt{n}} $$ where \(n = 10\).

🧮 Calculations

1. Calculate the Period

  • Period of one oscillation: $$ T = \frac{\overline{T_{10}}}{10} $$
  • Uncertainty in \(T\): $$ \Delta T = \frac{\Delta \overline{T_{10}}}{10} $$

2. Determine \(g\)

Use the formula for the period of a simple pendulum: $$ T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}} \Rightarrow g = \frac{4\pi^2 L}{T^2} $$

3. Propagate Uncertainties

Uncertainty in \(g\): $$ \Delta g = g \sqrt{\left( \frac{\Delta L}{L} \right)^2 + \left( 2 \cdot \frac{\Delta T}{T} \right)^2} $$

📊 Analysis

  1. Compare your measured \(g\) with the standard value:

  2. \(g \approx 9.81 \, \text{m/s}^2\)

  3. Discuss:

  4. The effect of instrument resolution on \(\Delta L\)
  5. Variability in timing and its impact on \(\Delta T\)
  6. Any assumptions or experimental limitations

📝 Deliverables

  1. Tabulated data:
  2. \(L\), \(\Delta L\), \(\overline{T_{10}}\), \(\sigma_{T_{10}}\), \(\Delta \overline{T_{10}}\), \(T\), \(\Delta T\)
  3. Calculated \(g\) and \(\Delta g\)
  4. Discussion of sources of uncertainty and their impact
